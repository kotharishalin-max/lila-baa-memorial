# Junu To Thayu Re Deval — The Temple Has Grown Old

Part of [[Lila Baa Besnu]] — track 08 of 25.

**Audio:** [Track 08 (S3)](s3view://public/lila-baa/audio/08-junu-to-thayu-re-deval.mp3)

One of Meerabai's best-known bhajans on the transience of the body, carried into Gujarati tradition as a memorial standard.

## Romanized (English script)

Junu to thayu re deval, junu to thayu,
Maaro hansalo nano ne deval junu to thayu.

Aare kaaya re hansa, dolvane lagi,
Padi gaya danta maayali rekhu to rahi,
Maaro hansalo nano ne deval junu to thayu.

Taare ne maare hansa prityu re bandhani,
Udi gayo hans pinjar padi to rahyu,
Maaro hansalo nano ne deval junu to thayu.

Baai Meera kahe Prabhu Giridhar-na gun,
Prem no pyalo tamne paavu ne pivu,
Maaro hansalo nano ne deval junu to thayu.

## Meaning

Meerabai's most famous bhajan on the temporariness of the body. The refrain frames the whole song: *"The temple has grown old, old indeed; my swan is still small, and yet the temple has grown old."* The swan (hansalo) is the undying soul; the temple/deval is the body. The second verse describes the body's aging — it begins to tremble; the teeth have fallen, leaving only the hollow line of the gums. The third verse is the decisive one: *"You and I, my swan, had bound ourselves in love; the swan flew away, and the cage was left behind."* Meera signs off with her signature — *"Bai Meera says: sing the qualities of Lord Giridhar; let us offer and drink the cup of love."* At a besnu, it names the truth around which the whole ceremony turns: the hansalo has flown, the deval is old, the bond of love is not what perished.
